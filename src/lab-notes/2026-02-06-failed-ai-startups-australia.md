## Overview
This research documents Australian AI startups that attracted attention, funding, or early traction but ultimately failed. Each entry includes public evidence of shutdown, administration, or liquidation. Acqui-hires and successful pivots are excluded.

---

## Hivery (Sydney)

**What it was:** Retail AI platform optimising product ranging and assortment decisions.

**Why it looked promising:**
- Founded in 2012
- Raised approximately A$60M
- Backed by Blackbird Ventures
- Enterprise retail customers

**Failure:** Entered voluntary administration in 2024 after running out of cash.

**Proof:**
- Australian Financial Review (2024): https://www.afr.com/technology/we-gave-it-everything-ai-start-up-calls-it-quits-after-raising-60m-20241113-p5kq51

---

## Flamingo AI (Melbourne)

**What it was:** Enterprise AI-powered customer service chatbot platform.

**Why it looked promising:**
- Raised approximately A$9M
- Enterprise pilots
- Early AI customer support positioning

**Failure:** Entered administration in 2023.

**Proof:**
- StartupDaily (2023): https://www.startupdaily.net/topic/artificial-intelligence/flamingo-ai-enters-administration/

## PredictiveHire (Sydney)

**What it was:** AI-driven recruitment assessment and psychometric testing platform.

**Why it looked promising:**
- Venture-backed
- Strong early HR adoption

**Failure:** Entered voluntary administration in 2023.

**Proof:**
- Australian Financial Review (2023): https://www.afr.com/work-and-careers/workplace/ai-hiring-start-up-predictivehire-enters-administration-20230822-p5dy8x

## Replica Studios (Brisbane)

**What it was:** AI-generated voice platform for games and creative industries.

**Why it looked promising:**
- Strong adoption in indie game development
- Ethical voice AI positioning

**Failure:** Public shutdown announced; services discontinued on 30 June 2025.

**Proof:**
- Multilingual.com (2025): https://multilingual.com/replica-studios-shutdown-2025/

---

## Analysis & Patterns

Common failure themes:
- AI differentiation eroded by foundation models
- High compute costs and weak margins
- Limited enterprise willingness to pay
- Regulatory and ethical constraints
- Small domestic market scale
